Pashupatinath Temple

Click here to goto main page

• • Details: History: The temple is believed to have been built in the 5th century and has undergone several renovations over the centuries. The current temple structure was built in the 15th century by the Malla kings of Nepal. The temple is located on the banks of the Bagmati River and is surrounded by several smaller temples and ashrams. • Architecture: The temple is known for its pagoda-style architecture and is made of wood and brick with a gilded roof. The main shrine houses a lingam, which is a symbol of Lord Shiva, and is considered to be one of the most sacred shrines in the world. The temple complex also has several other shrines and statues dedicated to various Hindu gods and goddesses. • Significance: Pashupatinath Temple is considered to be one of the 12 Jyotirlingas, which are believed to be the most sacred abodes of Lord Shiva. The temple is also a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is an important pilgrimage site for Hindus from all over the world. It is believed that those who die in the vicinity of the temple are liberated from the cycle of rebirth and attain moksha (liberation). • Festivals: The temple celebrates several festivals throughout the year, the most important of which is the Maha Shivaratri, which falls in the month of February or March. During this festival, devotees offer prayers and perform rituals to Lord Shiva and the lingam in the main shrine. • Visiting hours: The temple is open to Hindus from 4:00 am to 7:00 pm and to non-Hindus from 9:00 am to 5:00 pm every day. Visitors are required to adhere to the dress code and other rules and regulations. Non-Hindus are not allowed inside the main shrine. • The Pashupatinath Temple is an important religious and cultural site for Hindus and is a must-visit destination for anyone interested in Hindu spirituality and architecture. Its stunning pagoda-style architecture, rich history, and religious significance make it a unique and fascinating place to visit. • The exact date of the temple's construction is uncertain, but the current form of the temple was constructed in 1692 CE.[2] Over time, many more temples have been erected around the two-storied temple, including the Vaishnava temple complex with a Rama temple from the 14th century and the Guhyeshwari Temple mentioned in an 11th-century manuscript. • • Pashupatinath Temple is the oldest Hindu temple in Kathmandu. It is not known for certain when Pashupatinath Temple was built. But according to Nepal Mahatmaya and Himvatkhanda,[3] the deity here gained great fame there as Pashupati. Pashupatinath Temple's existence is recorded as early as 400 CE.[4] The ornamented pagoda houses the linga of Shiva. There are many legends describing how the temple of Aalok Pashupatinath came into existence here. • • One legend says that Shiva and Parvati took the form of antelopes in the forest on the Bagmati river's east bank. The gods later caught up with him and grabbed him by one of his horns, forcing him to resume his divine form. The broken horn was worshipped as a linga, but over time it was buried and lost. Centuries later a herdsman found one of his cows showering the earth with milk, and after digging at the site, he discovered the divine linga of Pashupatinath. • • According to Gopalraj Aalok Vhat, the temple was built by Prachanda Deva, a Licchavi king. • • Another chronicle states that Pashupatinath Temple was in the form of Linga shaped Devalaya before Supuspa Deva constructed a five-storey temple of Pashupatinath in this place. As time passed, the temple needed to be repaired and renovated. It is known that this temple was reconstructed by a medieval king named Shivadeva (1099–1126 CE). It was renovated by Ananta Malla adding a roof to it.[5][6] • • The main temple complex of Pashupatinath and the sanctum sanctorum was left untouched, but some of the outer buildings in the complex were damaged by the April 2015 Nepal earthquake.
